# Impact Model (CIDS)

Every action in Green Goods follows the **CIDS Framework** — a structured chain from raw activity to measurable impact. This ensures that work submissions are not just recorded, but contribute to verifiable outcomes.

## The CIDS Chain

```
Activity → Output → Outcome → Impact
```

| Stage | Definition | Example |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| **Activity** | The work performed | "Planted 12 mango trees" |
| **Output** | The direct product | "12 trees in the ground, geotagged" |
| **Outcome** | The change produced | "Increased canopy cover by 15% in zone A" |
| **Impact** | The long-term effect | "Carbon sequestration, improved biodiversity, food security" |

Every action schema in Green Goods must maintain this chain. Removing any link (especially evidence capture at the Activity stage) breaks the verification model.

## Action Chain Examples

### Planting

```
Activity: Plant trees/crops in designated area
  → Output: Number of plants, species, GPS coordinates, photo evidence
    → Outcome: Increased vegetation cover, soil health improvement
      → Impact: Carbon sequestration, biodiversity, food security
```

### Harvesting

```
Activity: Harvest crops from garden plots
  → Output: Harvest weight/volume, crop type, quality assessment
    → Outcome: Food produced for community consumption or sale
      → Impact: Food security, economic value, sustainable agriculture
```

### Trash Pick Ups

```
Activity: Collect and sort waste from designated areas
  → Output: Weight/volume of waste, categories (plastic, organic, metal)
    → Outcome: Reduced environmental pollution, materials diverted from landfill
      → Impact: Cleaner ecosystems, recyclable material recovery, community health
```

### Tree Observation

```
Activity: Monitor and document tree health and growth
  → Output: Growth measurements, health indicators, photo documentation
    → Outcome: Longitudinal data on reforestation progress
      → Impact: Scientific evidence base, adaptive management, carbon tracking
```

### Ecological Maintenance

```
Activity: Maintain garden infrastructure (irrigation, fencing, composting)
  → Output: Maintenance tasks completed, materials used, area covered
    → Outcome: Sustained garden productivity, reduced degradation
      → Impact: Long-term ecosystem health, resource efficiency
```

## Action Domains

Actions are grouped into five domains, each with specific impact metrics:

### Solar Infrastructure

**Impact metrics:** kWh generated, panels maintained, outages prevented

Solar monitoring actions track installation, maintenance, and performance of community solar systems. Particularly relevant for communities with unreliable grid power.

### Waste Management

**Impact metrics:** kg diverted from landfill, recycling rate, area cleaned

Waste actions cover collection, sorting, and processing. Integrates with community currency systems (e.g., Sarafu) where waste collection earns local credits.

### Agroforestry

**Impact metrics:** trees planted, species diversity, canopy cover, harvest yield

Agroforestry actions span planting, maintenance, observation, and harvest. This domain produces the richest scientific data and supports DeSci partnerships.

### Education

**Impact metrics:** students engaged, curricula completed, knowledge assessments

Education actions track learning activities tied to environmental stewardship. Includes school-based programs where students adopt and monitor trees.

### Mutual Credit

**Impact metrics:** transactions facilitated, community participation rate, economic velocity

Mutual credit actions capture community economic activity powered by local exchange systems. Tracks how environmental work connects to local economic resilience.

## Core vs Domain-Specific Actions

| Type | Availability | Examples |
|------|-------------|----------|
| **Core actions** (7) | Available in ALL gardens | Plant, Harvest, Clean Up, Observe, Maintain, Water, Compost |
| **Domain-specific** | Opt-in per garden | Solar Panel Install, Waste Sort, Tree Health Survey |

Garden operators configure which domain-specific actions their community needs. Core actions are always available and cannot be removed.

## Schema Structure

Every action has a schema registered on-chain via the Action Registry. Schemas define:

- **Required fields**: What evidence the gardener must provide (photos, measurements, GPS)
- **Validation rules**: What the operator checks during review
- **Assessment criteria**: What the evaluator uses for certification
- **Impact mapping**: How the output connects to CIDS outcomes

## Eight Forms of Capital

Assessments in Green Goods are framed using the **Eight Forms of Capital**, ensuring impact is measured beyond just financial value:

| Capital Form | What It Measures |
|-------------|-----------------|
| **Natural** | Ecosystem health, biodiversity, soil quality |
| **Social** | Community bonds, trust networks, collective action |
| **Financial** | Economic value generated, costs saved |
| **Material** | Physical infrastructure created or maintained |
| **Living** | Food produced, species supported, health outcomes |
| **Intellectual** | Knowledge generated, research data, curricula |
| **Experiential** | Skills developed, practices learned |
| **Spiritual/Cultural** | Cultural preservation, sense of purpose, connection to land |

## Next Steps

The CIDS model is evolving. Planned improvements include:

- **Cross-action correlation**: Linking related actions (e.g., planting → observation → harvest) into impact narratives
- **Proportional contribution**: Attributing outcomes to multiple gardeners who contributed to the same area
- **Temporal tracking**: Connecting point-in-time actions to longitudinal outcomes over seasons and years
