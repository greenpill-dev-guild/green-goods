# Communities & Localization

Green Goods is not an abstract platform — it serves **20+ active garden communities** across Latin America, Africa, and North America. Every feature decision should be evaluated against the real-world constraints these communities face.

## Who is Green Goods for?

Green Goods brings together five roles, each contributing to the impact cycle:

- **Gardener** — Documents regenerative work in the field. Takes photos, records observations, and submits evidence of completed actions.
- **Operator** — Manages garden communities and reviews submissions. Sets up actions, approves work, and coordinates the team.
- **Evaluator** — Verifies impact data and produces analysis. Queries across gardens, validates evidence chains, and creates reports for funders.
- **Community Member** — Participates in governance and local coordination. Contributes to decision-making through signal pools and community proposals.
- **Funder** — Supports verified impact through vault deposits and hypercert purchases. Backs regenerative work with capital and receives transparent reporting on outcomes.

Each role has its own guide — see [Gardener](/gardener/get-started), [Operator](/operator/get-started-and-roles), and [Evaluator](/evaluator/get-started) to get started.

## Active Garden Communities

### Geographic Distribution

Green Goods communities are concentrated in three regions:

| Region | Communities | Primary Languages |
|--------|------------|-------------------|
| **Latin America** | ~12 communities | Portuguese, Spanish |
| **Africa** | ~5 communities | English, Swahili, French |
| **North America** | ~3 communities | English, Spanish |

### Community Characteristics

Most garden communities share common traits:

- **Team size:** 5–30 active gardeners per garden
- **Connectivity:** Intermittent (2G/3G common, Wi-Fi rare in field)
- **Devices:** Android smartphones (mid-range), some feature phones
- **Technical literacy:** Low to moderate (operators are more technical)
- **Reporting cadence:** Weekly to bi-weekly work submissions

## Community Spotlights

### University of Nigeria Nsukka — Solar Infrastructure

A university-based community monitoring and maintaining solar panel installations. Power outages are common, making solar infrastructure critical for daily operations.

**Key context for development:**
- Offline-first is essential — power outages mean both connectivity and device charging are unreliable
- Solar monitoring actions track panel health, energy output, and maintenance needs
- Academic partnerships generate research data alongside operational impact

### Cape Town — Waste Management with Sarafu

A waste collection community integrating with the Sarafu community currency network. Waste collectors earn Sarafu credits for verified collection work.

**Key context for development:**
- Mutual credit integration means actions have immediate economic value for gardeners
- Waste sorting categories must match local recycling infrastructure
- Multi-language support (English + local languages) is critical

### AgroforestryDAO Brasil — Agroforestry + DeSci

A Brazilian agroforestry collective combining practical fieldwork with decentralized science (DeSci) data partnerships. Generates scientific datasets from tree observations and harvest tracking.

**Key context for development:**
- Portuguese is the primary language — English-only UX is a hard blocker
- Scientific data quality matters — observation schemas need precision fields (species, DBH, canopy cover)
- DeSci partnerships require data export in standardized formats

### Uganda — School-Tree-Student Program

A school-based program where students adopt and monitor trees as part of their curriculum. Each student is responsible for a specific tree and submits regular observations.

**Key context for development:**
- Mobile-first, low bandwidth — page weight and image compression matter
- Education domain actions track student engagement alongside environmental outcomes
- Batch operations are important (teachers manage submissions for entire classes)

## Localization Strategy

### Language Tiers

| Tier | Languages | Status | Coverage |
|------|-----------|--------|----------|
| **Tier 1** | English, Portuguese, Spanish | Active | Full UI, all action schemas, documentation |
| **Tier 2** | French, Swahili | Planned | Core UI, common action schemas |
| **Tier 3** | Arabic, Hausa | Future | Core submission flow only |

### Localization Principles

1. **UI strings are externalized** — All user-facing text uses `react-intl` message descriptors, never hardcoded strings
2. **Action schemas are localizable** — Action names, descriptions, and field labels support per-locale overrides
3. **Right-to-left (RTL) support** — Tier 3 languages (Arabic) will require RTL layout support
4. **Cultural sensitivity** — Impact framing varies by community (carbon language resonates in some contexts, food security in others)

## Garden Operator Survey Insights

Based on operator feedback from active communities:

### Typical Workflows

- Operators spend **2–4 hours/week** on garden management
- Most time is spent on **work review** (approving/rejecting submissions)
- Batch operations are the most requested feature improvement
- Reporting to funders is the most painful manual process

### Tools Currently Used

- **WhatsApp groups** for gardener coordination (pre-Green Goods)
- **Google Sheets** for tracking submissions (being replaced)
- **Email** for funder reporting (target for automation)
- **Green Goods admin** for on-chain operations

### Pain Points

- Reviewing work one-at-a-time is slow for gardens with 20+ gardeners
- Explaining Hypercerts to funders requires too much blockchain context
- No way to see "garden health" at a glance (are gardeners active? is the vault growing?)
- Language barriers when the platform defaults to English
