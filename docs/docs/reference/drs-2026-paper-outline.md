# Toward a Regenerative Digital Design Framework: Principles and Practice from Green Goods

**Submission target**: DRS 2026 — Regenerative Design Track
**Paper type**: Research-through-Design / Case Study
**Authors**: Greenpill Dev Guild

---

## Abstract (~250 words)

No authoritative framework exists for applying regenerative design principles to digital products and interaction design. While regenerative development has matured in the built environment (Mang & Reed, 2016) and agriculture (Savory, Brown), its translation to software and interface design remains largely metaphorical. This paper presents a seven-principle Regenerative Digital Design Framework synthesized from 11 intellectual traditions — living systems theory, commons governance, biomimicry, regenerative economics, indigenous epistemologies, solarpunk aesthetics, mycorrhizal finance, eudaimonic motivation design, and the meta-crisis coordination literature — and operationalized through Green Goods, an offline-first platform for documenting, verifying, and funding community-led regenerative work across four countries. We demonstrate that regenerative design in digital contexts is not the application of ecological metaphors to screens but a fundamentally different orientation: designing for capability over engagement, self-governance over central control, ecological rhythms over manufactured urgency, and community self-sufficiency over platform dependency. The framework addresses legitimate critiques (vagueness, greenwashing risk, measurement challenges, cultural appropriation) through specific, auditable design principles. We contribute the first published framework for regenerative interaction design and evidence from a live platform operating across Nigeria, South Africa, Brazil, and Kenya.

**Keywords**: regenerative design, digital design framework, commons governance, biomimicry, living systems, platform cooperativism, post-growth HCI, indigenous design epistemology

## 1. Introduction (~1500 words)

### 1.1 The Gap
- No authoritative digital/UX regenerative design framework exists (DRS 2026 is the first dedicated track)
- Regenerative design has matured in built environment and agriculture but not in digital/interaction design
- The few attempts remain metaphorical (nature-themed aesthetics) rather than functionally regenerative
- Post-growth HCI (Knowles et al., 2023) critiques engagement-driven design but offers limited constructive alternatives

### 1.2 The Opportunity
- Digital platforms mediate increasingly critical coordination (climate, public goods, community governance)
- Existing design paradigms (user-centered, human-centered) center the individual user, not the web of relationships
- Regenerative approaches from ecology, economics, and indigenous knowledge offer different design ontologies
- Green Goods provides a concrete case study: a live platform operationalizing these principles across 4 countries

### 1.3 Research Question
How can regenerative design principles — developed for the built environment, agriculture, and economics — be translated into an actionable framework for digital product and interaction design? What does a regenerative digital design framework look like in practice?

### 1.4 Contribution
- First published framework for regenerative digital interaction design
- Seven principles synthesized from 11 traditions, with auditable checklists
- Evidence from a live platform (Green Goods) serving gardeners, operators, evaluators, funders, and community members
- Honest engagement with critiques (vagueness, greenwashing, cultural appropriation, measurement)

## 2. Related Work (~3000 words)

### 2.1 Regenerative Design in the Built Environment
- Reed (2007) "Shifting from Sustainability to Regeneration" — the trajectory model
- Mang & Reed (2012, 2016) — Story of Place, four premises, Essential Living Processes
- Regenesis Group methodology — case studies (Playa Viva, Las Salinas)
- Living Building Challenge — Brock Environmental Center as regenerative exemplar
- **Gap**: all geographic/physical, no digital translation

### 2.2 Living Systems Theory
- Capra (1996) — autopoiesis, dissipative structures, Santiago Theory of Cognition
- Meadows (1999) — twelve leverage points
- Beer (1972) — Viable System Model
- Alexander (2002-2005) — Nature of Order, living structure, wholeness and centers
- **Gap**: applied to organizational design but not interface design

### 2.3 Commons Governance
- Ostrom (1990) — 8 design principles for managing commons
- Bollier & Helfrich (2019) — commoning as verb, Triad of Commoning
- Hess & Ostrom (2007) — knowledge as commons
- Allen — revised Ostrom principles for digital contexts
- Commons Stack — automating Ostrom through conviction voting
- **Gap**: governance mechanisms exist but interface design implications are underexplored

### 2.4 Biomimicry in Digital Design
- Benyus (1997) — three levels (form, process, ecosystem)
- Biomimicry Institute — Life's Principles (26 sub-principles)
- ACM DIS 2020 — "The Nature of Biodesigned Systems" workshop
- Mycorrhizal networks — reciprocal exchange, not generous communism (Simard, 2016)
- **Gap**: mostly at Level 1 (form); process and ecosystem levels underdeveloped for digital

### 2.5 Regenerative Economics and Post-Growth HCI
- Raworth (2017) — Doughnut Economics, seven paradigm shifts
- Knowles et al. (2023) — post-growth HCI (ACM TOCHI)
- Scholz (2016) — platform cooperativism
- Felber — Economy for the Common Good
- **Gap**: post-growth HCI critiques but offers limited constructive framework

### 2.6 Indigenous Design Epistemologies
- Page & Memmott (2021) — Country as design partner, Deep Listening
- Te Aranga Maori Design Principles — outcome-based, embedded in Auckland planning
- Marshall — Two-Eyed Seeing (Etuaptmumk)
- Kimmerer (2013) — Honorable Harvest, reciprocity
- Yunkaporta (2019) — pattern thinking, us-two, four protocols
- Costanza-Chock (2020) — Design Justice
- Wilson (2008) — relational accountability
- **Gap**: rich frameworks exist but have not been synthesized into digital design guidance

### 2.7 Motivation Design
- Chou (2015) — Octalysis, White Hat vs Black Hat drives
- Deterding (2014) — eudaimonic design, autonomy as key factor
- Bogost (2011) — gamification as exploitationware
- Nicholson (2015) — RECIPE for meaningful gamification
- Deci & Ryan (1985) — Self-Determination Theory
- **Gap**: no application to regenerative or commons contexts

### 2.8 The Meta-Crisis and Coordination
- Schmachtenberger — metacrisis, generator functions, multipolar traps, third attractor
- Alexander (2014) — "Meditations on Moloch" (coordination failure personified)
- Owocki (2022) — GreenPilled, slaying Moloch through mechanism design
- Emmett & Zartler (2023) — MycoFi, mycorrhizal finance patterns

## 3. Framework: Seven Principles of Regenerative Digital Design (~3000 words)

### 3.1 Synthesis Methodology
- 11 traditions reviewed (living systems, commons, biomimicry, economics, indigenous, motivation, meta-crisis, Greenpill, MycoFi, solarpunk, post-growth HCI)
- Convergence analysis: what do independent traditions agree on?
- Translation: from built environment / ecology / economics to digital interaction design
- Validation: does Green Goods (live platform, 4 countries) already implement these?

### 3.2 The Seven Principles
For each principle: definition, theoretical grounding (which traditions converge), design directive, auditable checklist item, and Green Goods implementation.

1. **Make the Mycelium Visible** (MycoFi + mycorrhizal ecology + Raworth distributive design)
2. **Design for Succession** (ecological succession + Octalysis phases + Greenpill chapter maturity)
3. **Enrich the Edges** (edge effects in ecology + innovation at boundaries)
4. **Failure is Succession** (ecological resilience + Living Building Challenge)
5. **Be Growth-Agnostic** (Raworth + post-growth HCI + SDT)
6. **Capability is the Deliverable** (Regenesis + eudaimonic design + Walkaway Test)
7. **Regen, Not Degen** (Solarpunk + Greenpill + degen/regen cultural distinction)

### 3.3 Anti-Patterns
The Degen/Regen Pattern Table as design anti-pattern reference.

## 4. Case Study: Green Goods (~3000 words)

### 4.1 Platform Description
- Offline-first PWA for documenting, verifying, and funding regenerative work
- Five-sided marketplace: gardeners, operators, evaluators, funders, community
- On-chain attestation chain: work → approval → assessment → hypercert → funding
- Conviction voting governance, Hats Protocol roles, ERC-6551 tokenbound gardens
- Deployed across Nigeria, South Africa, Brazil, Kenya

## 5. Discussion (~2000 words)

### 5.1 What Regenerative Design Is NOT in Digital Contexts
- Not nature-themed aesthetics (leaf icons, green palettes)
- Not sustainability metrics bolted onto conventional UX
- Not gamification for engagement with green branding
- Not "ethical design" repackaged with ecological vocabulary

### 5.2 What Regenerative Design IS
- A fundamentally different orientation: designing for capability, self-governance, ecological rhythms, and community self-sufficiency
- Working at Meadows' leverage points 4-2 (self-organization, goals, paradigm), not 12-10 (parameters, buffers)
- Commons governance encoded in interaction patterns
- Motivation design serving flourishing, not dependency

### 5.3 Engaging the Critiques
- **Vagueness**: The seven principles provide auditable specificity
- **Greenwashing risk**: Community-based verification, multi-stakeholder accountability
- **Measurement**: Honest distinction between activity verification and impact verification
- **Cultural appropriation**: Attribution practices, Two-Eyed Seeing as methodology
- **Complexity trap**: Complexity in architecture, simplicity in experience
- **Efficiency tension**: Explicit tradeoff documentation (where we chose regenerative over efficient)

### 5.4 Limitations
- Single platform case study
- Framework derived from theoretical synthesis, not empirical validation of outcomes
- No longitudinal data on whether regenerative design principles produce measurably different outcomes
- The meta-crisis framing may be too abstract for practitioner adoption

## 6. Conclusion (~500 words)

- First published regenerative digital design framework
- Seven principles grounded in 11 traditions, operationalized in a live platform
- The framework's job is to name what Green Goods already does implicitly — making implicit principles explicit for teaching, auditing, and defense
- Future work: empirical validation, cross-platform application, longitudinal impact measurement
- Invitation to the DRS community: this is an open framework; contribute, critique, fork
