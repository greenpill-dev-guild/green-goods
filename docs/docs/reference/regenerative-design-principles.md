# Seven Principles of Regenerative Digital Design

A concise reference for the Greenpill Dev Guild and broader regenerative builder community. These principles guide every design decision in Green Goods — and can guide yours.

> Full framework with citations: [Regenerative Design Framework](/reference/regenerative-design-framework)

---

## 1. Make the Mycelium Visible

**Show value flows end-to-end.**

In mycorrhizal networks, fungi redistribute nutrients between trees — Mother Trees share with seedlings, flows shift toward need, and resources move from rich patches to poor ones. Green Goods does the same: verified work attracts funding, yield routes from vaults to gardens, conviction voting shifts allocation to current need. The difference from a forest floor: **a digital platform can make these flows visible.** Show every participant how their action connects to the whole.

*Grounded in: Suzanne Simard (Mother Trees), Jeff Emmett (MyCoFi), Kate Raworth (distributive design)*

---

## 2. Design for Succession

**Match interface complexity to community maturity.**

Ecosystems progress through stages: pioneer (lichens on rock), intermediate (grasses building soil), climax (mature forest). Platform communities do the same. A new garden with 3 gardeners needs templates and quick wins. A mature garden with 20+ active members needs advanced governance and cross-garden features. Don't design one interface for all stages.

*Grounded in: Ecological succession, Greenpill chapter maturity model (L0-L3), Octalysis motivation phases*

---

## 3. Enrich the Edges

**Invest disproportionate design attention at stakeholder boundaries.**

In ecology, ecotones (transition zones between ecosystems) produce the greatest biodiversity. In Green Goods, the richest design zones are the gardener-operator boundary (review queue), the funder-garden boundary (vault interface), and the web3/non-web3 boundary (passkey onboarding). Don't smooth these edges over — enrich them.

*Grounded in: Edge effects in ecology, May East (sociotone), innovation at boundaries*

---

## 4. Failure is Succession

**Frame all "negative" states as opportunities with context.**

Ecosystems don't prevent failure — they prepare for it as a generative event. The Lodgepole Pine carries cones that open only during fire. When work is rejected, show what the operator saw and what could improve. When connectivity is lost, design for dormancy, not error. When an operator leaves, design for smooth handover. Rejection is clearing. Disconnection is dormancy. Disturbance triggers renewal.

*Grounded in: Ecological resilience, Biomimicry Life's Principles, Living Building Challenge*

---

## 5. Be Growth-Agnostic

**Measure impact depth, not engagement breadth.**

A garden sustaining 15 gardeners doing consistent verified work is successful. No streaks. No re-engagement notifications. No FOMO. Celebrate task completion and departure: "Your work is queued. Go plant another tree." Measure verified regenerative impact per unit of gardener effort — not daily active users.

*Grounded in: Kate Raworth (Doughnut Economics), Post-growth HCI (ACM TOCHI 2023), Trebor Scholz (platform cooperativism)*

---

## 6. Capability is the Deliverable

**Build community independence, not platform dependency.**

The deliverable of regenerative design is capability — the power to evolve from understanding. Every feature must pass the Walkaway Test: if Green Goods disappeared tomorrow, could the community continue its work? Recognition badges mark capability transitions ("Can train others"), not activity volume ("100 submissions"). The Endgame is community self-sufficiency.

*Grounded in: Regenesis Group (developmental capacity), Deterding (eudaimonic design), Ethereum Foundation Mandate (Walkaway Test)*

---

## 7. Regen, Not Degen

**Every visual and interaction choice is a philosophical statement.**

| Degen | Regen |
|-------|-------|
| Dark mode, PnL red/green | Natural light, earth tones |
| Countdown timers | Seasonal rhythms |
| Dopamine notifications | Celebration of departure |
| Individual portfolios | Garden-level collective view |
| "Number go up" | "Capacity to regenerate grows" |
| Financial terminal | Garden journal |

*Grounded in: Solarpunk Manifesto ("We're gardeners"), Greenpill degen/regen distinction*

## The Meta-Crisis Connection

These principles are not decorative. They address Daniel Schmachtenberger's metacrisis — the convergence of existential risks sharing common generator functions. The core generator is **multipolar traps**: game-theoretic situations where rational self-interest produces collective destruction. The Greenpill community calls this Moloch. Green Goods is a coordination mechanism designed to slay Moloch: it makes defection costly (unverified work gets no funding) and cooperation rewarding (verified work attracts capital). Each principle is a design decision that favors coordination over extraction.

## Quick Audit

Before shipping any new feature or view:

- [ ] Can the user trace how their action connects to the larger system? *(Mycelium)*
- [ ] Is this feature appropriate for the garden's succession stage? *(Succession)*
- [ ] If at a stakeholder boundary, is it designed for learning? *(Edges)*
- [ ] Do negative states guide toward renewal with context? *(Failure)*
- [ ] Could removing this increase engagement? If yes, good. *(Growth-agnostic)*
- [ ] Does this build independence or create dependency? *(Capability)*
- [ ] Would this feel at home in a solarpunk world? *(Regen)*
