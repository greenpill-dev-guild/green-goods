# OSV Application Draft

## Submission Setup

- **application type**: Group application
- **applicant group**: Greenpill Dev Guild
- **lead**: Afolabi Aiyeloja
- **flagship project**: Green Goods
- **scope**: 9-month last-mile pilot for agentic impact reporting
- **build window**: months 1-2
- **pilot start**: month 3
- **first pilot**: Nigeria solar maintenance
- **fallback / second pilot**: Cape Town waste collection

## Core Thesis

Green Goods exists to make it possible for people doing ecological and civic work to prove that work with tools they already have, even in low-connectivity environments, so their communities can earn trust and funding.

## Group Narrative

Greenpill Dev Guild is the public-goods builder collective behind Green Goods. Afolabi Aiyeloja leads the work as the lead steward, systems architect, and principal builder. The guild gives the project execution depth across design, engineering, product, and ecosystem support, while Green Goods is the flagship product where that vision becomes usable infrastructure.

The application should make the hierarchy clear:

- **Greenpill Dev Guild** = the applicant group
- **Afolabi** = the lead steward, systems architect, and principal builder
- **Green Goods** = the flagship product
- **pilot communities** = the proving ground

## What Is Already Real

The application should speak from current reality first.

Green Goods already has:

- an offline-first PWA for documenting work
- multilingual support in English, Spanish, and Portuguese
- passkey and wallet onboarding paths
- operator review and approval workflows
- EAS-based attestation flows
- Hypercert and funding infrastructure in the broader product architecture
- an agent package that already supports text and voice submission patterns

This is not a request to fund a vague idea. It is a request to help push an existing body of work into a live, human-centered pilot.

## Draft Answers

### 1. What are you working on?

We are building a simple way for people doing tree care, waste collection, solar maintenance, and other public goods work to document what they did with the tools they already use, even offline. That proof helps communities coordinate better and earn trust and funding.

Alternate shorter version:

We are building a low-bandwidth reporting system that lets people prove real-world public goods work with WhatsApp, SMS, voice, and simple mobile flows, so their communities can turn invisible labor into trusted evidence and support.

### 2. Why are you the best people to work on this? What is novel in your approach?

This project sits at the intersection of product design, public goods coordination, and field realities. I lead Greenpill Dev Guild, a collective building open infrastructure for regenerative communities, and Green Goods is our flagship attempt to make important work legible instead of invisible. What is novel about our approach is not putting impact on a blockchain. It is making proof of work accessible at the last mile with tools people already use, while keeping the resulting evidence portable, reviewable, and useful to both communities and funders. We are building from real partner contexts in Nigeria, Cape Town, Uganda, and Brazil, so the design starts from unreliable connectivity, multilingual workflows, and operator trust, not from idealized users.

### 3. What could success look like 1 year from now?

One year from now, success looks like Greenpill Dev Guild running a live, repeatable reporting loop in real communities where field workers can submit work by voice, text, or simple mobile flows, operators can review that work quickly, and the result is clear enough for funders and researchers to trust. The first success is not scale for its own sake. It is proving that communities with limited connectivity can produce high-quality evidence without needing grant writers or crypto-native tools. Beyond our wildest dreams, Green Goods becomes the default public infrastructure layer for communities doing regenerative and civic work, and invisible labor starts becoming visible, portable, and fundable everywhere.

### 4. What might failure look like?

Failure would look like building something technically impressive that communities do not trust or use. The biggest original mistakes would be overbuilding the protocol layer, making the AI clever but unreliable, asking operators to do too much manual cleanup, or designing for demo conditions instead of field conditions. Another failure mode would be using language and workflows that make sense to funders or crypto builders but not to the people doing the work. The project only succeeds if it feels simpler than today's alternatives, not more complex.

### 5. List 3 of your most exceptional achievements

- **Green Goods v1 launch** proves Afolabi can take a complex public-goods idea and ship a real system.
- **GG24 Public Goods Tooling Round** proves he can operate credible funding and ecosystem coordination, not just build software in isolation.
- **Regenerative Stack article series** proves he can turn strategy into language that moves builders, funders, and communities toward the same vision.

### 6. Describe something that you find beautiful in the world

I find it beautiful when a community learns how to recognize its own care. A planted tree, a repaired solar panel, a cleaned shoreline, a student tending something living - these acts are small on their own, but together they become memory, trust, and shared meaning. Good tools can help a community see that its care is real and worth building around.
